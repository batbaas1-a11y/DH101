# Week 8 – Networks of Knowledge & Power

## The Artifact

This network visualizes the global AI supply chain, showing relationships between raw materials, countries, technology companies, infrastructure, and AI systems.

[View my interactive network on Kumu](https://kumu.io/sara0420/make-7#networks-of-knowledge-and-power)

## Process Notes
I created this network using Kumu to map relationships in the AI supply chain. I started by identifying key categories: raw materials, countries, manufacturing, technology companies, infrastructure, and AI systems. 

I then added nodes for each category and used color coding to distinguish between them. After that, I created connections that represent real-world relationships such as “supplies,” “depends on,” and “powers.” I also added descriptions to both nodes and connections to explain their roles in the network.

One important decision I made was to include hidden elements such as labor (miners), environmental impact, and energy consumption. This helped highlight the less visible aspects of AI systems that are often ignored.

Throughout the process, I adjusted the layout to make the network more readable and to emphasize central nodes like data centers and semiconductor chips.

## Reflection
This network visualizes the global AI supply chain and reveals how power is distributed across materials, infrastructure, and technology companies. The most central node in this network is data centers, because they connect multiple layers of the system, including tech companies, cloud computing, and AI models. Without data centers, AI systems cannot be trained or deployed, making them a key point where control over resources translates into control over knowledge and technology.

Several distinct clusters emerge in the network. One cluster includes raw materials and countries, such as cobalt, lithium, rare earth minerals, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Chile, and China. Another cluster consists of technology companies, including Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Nvidia, Apple, and Tesla. A third cluster focuses on infrastructure, including data centers, cloud computing, and AI models. Finally, there is a power layer, represented by OpenAI and broader networks of knowledge and power.

One surprising insight is that although major tech companies appear to dominate the network, they are highly dependent on resources and labor from other parts of the world. Countries in the Global South play a crucial role in supplying materials, yet they are often less visible in discussions about AI power.

What is missing from this network is equally important. While nodes such as labor (miners), environmental impact, and energy consumption are included, they remain peripheral compared to tech companies, reflecting how these factors are often overlooked.
