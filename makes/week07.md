# Week 7 – Mapping AI Worlds

## The Artifact
**Map Title:** [Powering AI: The Energy Infrastructure Behind Microsoft and Meta Data Centers](https://www.google.com/maps/d/u/0/edit?mid=1QE-LgplSV8vcufC7OeT0voyGa8Asm5A&ll=24.836857797000615%2C-9.225737949999996&z=3)

- This digital map visualizes the hidden infrastructure that powers artificial intelligence systems operated by Microsoft and Meta. While AI often appears to be purely digital, it relies on a vast network of physical systems including corporate headquarters, data centers, and large energy infrastructures such as solar farms, wind farms, nuclear plants, and water-based cooling systems.

The map highlights three layers of AI infrastructure:
- Corporate headquarters where decisions about AI systems are made
- Data centers where cloud computing and AI models are hosted
- Energy infrastructure that provides the electricity and cooling needed to run these systems

By mapping these locations together, the project reveals that AI depends heavily on global energy systems and environmental resources.

## Reflection
When mapping the infrastructure behind artificial intelligence, a clear pattern emerges: the power and control of AI are concentrated in a small number of technology companies, while the environmental and infrastructural costs are distributed across many regions. The headquarters of companies such as Microsoft and Meta are located in a few major technology hubs, but the data centers and energy systems that support them are spread across the United States and other parts of the world.

This map shows that AI depends on massive amounts of energy and physical infrastructure. Data centers require enormous amounts of electricity to power servers and cooling systems, which explains why they are often located near renewable energy sources such as wind farms or solar installations. These facilities also depend on water for cooling, which can place additional pressure on local environmental resources.

One important question raised by this map is who benefits from AI and who bears the costs. Technology companies benefit economically from artificial intelligence systems, but the environmental impact of energy consumption and resource use is often experienced by the communities where data centers and energy infrastructure are located.

At the same time, there are important elements missing from this map. The map does not show the labor involved in building hardware, mining minerals, or maintaining the infrastructure that supports AI systems. These hidden forms of labor are part of the larger global network that makes artificial intelligence possible.

From a Data Feminism perspective, mapping infrastructure like this helps reveal the hidden systems of power that support digital technologies. By visualizing the energy and environmental resources behind AI, the map challenges the idea that artificial intelligence exists purely in the digital world.
