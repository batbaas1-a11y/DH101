## Process Notes
For this project, I created a data visualization using Flourish to explore the carbon footprint of AI. I chose Track A (Carbon Footprint Visualization) because I was interested in understanding how AI’s environmental impact compares to everyday activities.

I used Flourish to build a bar chart and then turned it into a story with multiple slides to show how scale changes the meaning of the data. I collected data from course materials and external sources, including estimates for GPT-3 training emissions, a single ChatGPT query, a New York–London flight, and the annual carbon footprint of a U.S. household.

One of the main decisions I made was to use a logarithmic scale. The data values were extremely different in size, so a normal scale made the smaller values invisible. The log scale allowed me to show both very large and very small emissions in the same visualization.

Another key decision was to structure the visualization as a story rather than just a static chart. I started with a single AI query to make it seem small, then gradually expanded to show how emissions grow when scaled up. This helped make the data more understandable and impactful.

The biggest challenge was dealing with the differences in scale and making the visualization readable. It was also difficult to find consistent data for AI emissions, especially for individual queries, since estimates vary depending on the source and assumptions.

## Reflection (Artist Statement)
Before this project, I mostly thought of AI as something abstract and digital, without considering its physical and environmental impact. Researching the carbon footprint of AI changed how I think about everyday technology use. I was surprised by how large the emissions from training a model like GPT-3 are, especially when compared to things like flights or household energy use.

At the same time, I found it interesting that a single ChatGPT query has such a small footprint on its own. This made me realize that the real issue is not individual use, but scale. When millions or billions of people use AI systems every day, even very small emissions can add up to something significant.

This project made me reflect on my own AI use habits. I use AI frequently for schoolwork, brainstorming, and studying, and I don’t think I would completely stop using it because it is genuinely helpful. However, I am more aware now of when I might be using it unnecessarily. I would be willing to reduce using AI for very simple or repetitive tasks that I could do quickly on my own.

I also think that individual actions have limits. While users can be more mindful, the larger responsibility lies with companies that design and run these systems. They control the energy sources, infrastructure, and scale of AI. Overall, this project helped me understand that AI is not just a digital tool—it has real-world environmental consequences that need to be considered.
